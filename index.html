01 / Purpose

Why this matters

The Problem

The Calusa dominated South Florida for over a thousand years. They built shell mound cities, controlled trade routes across the Caribbean, and resisted Spanish colonization longer than any other group. Yet their language, the key to their identity, survives only as scattered fragments in 16th-century Spanish documents. Fewer than a dozen words have confirmed translations.

The Method

We use AI to cross-reference every available colonial-era document (memoirs, mission records, maps, legal correspondence) against neighboring indigenous languages. Computer-driven morphological analysis, phonetic matching, and vision-based manuscript reading extract vocabulary that human researchers might miss.

Voice Attribution

Audio pronunciation is modeled on recordings of Southeast US indigenous language speakers from the Muskogee (Creek/Seminole) Documentation Project at the College of William & Mary. Creek and Seminole are geographically and culturally the closest living languages to the extinct Calusa. Source recordings include Margaret McKane Mauldin reading Creek folktales and Spencer Frank broadcasting in Seminole on KWSH radio (1990-91). This project uses these materials for language revitalization research, not commercial purposes.

05 / Language Network

Connected languages

South Florida's languages existed in a network of trade, migration, and contact. We map relationships to find cross-linguistic patterns.

06 / Cross-Linguistic Matches

Shared roots and morphemes

By comparing Calusa words against Timucua, Taíno, Creek, and other documented languages, we identify shared morphemes that reveal meaning and origin.

07 / Tunica-Calusa Cognates

Testing Granberry's hypothesis

Julian Granberry proposed that Calusa is related to Tunica. Established cognate pairs from his published work are distinguished from AI-proposed hypotheses.
